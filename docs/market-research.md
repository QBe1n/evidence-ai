- **Annual SLR spend per major pharma company: ~$16.8M**
- **Annual SLR spend per major academic institution: ~$18.7M**

- There are approximately **20 major pharma companies** (by R&D spend) and **hundreds of mid-size and specialty biotech/pharma companies**

- Mid-size biotech/pharma typically commission 10–50 SLRs per product over its lifecycle; with ~500 products in active development at any given time, this adds substantial volume
- The broader universe (including academic institutions, government agencies, HTA bodies, CROs) multiplies this substantially

### 2.3 Regulatory Evidence Requirements

Every major FDA marketing application requires:
- **Module 2.5 (eCTD):** Clinical Overview — a 30-page narrative synthesizing all clinical evidence
- **Module 2.7 (eCTD):** Clinical Summary — detailed integrated summaries of safety and efficacy
- **Module 5 (eCTD):** Full study reports

For novel drugs, the Clinical Overview and Clinical Summary effectively constitute a systematic synthesis of all available published and unpublished literature on the compound. For devices, the 510(k) substantial equivalence argument and PMA clinical section serve similar roles.

Additionally, beyond FDA, every major product requires HTA submissions for reimbursement (NICE in UK, CADTH in Canada, ICER in US, regional agencies globally). **Each HTA submission typically requires 3 separate SLRs** (clinical evidence, economic evidence, quality of life/HRQoL), plus a cost-effectiveness model.

## 5. Pain Points and AI Leverage

### 5.1 What Makes Systematic Reviews Expensive and Slow

#### Volume and Precision Problem
A typical SLR starts with **5,000–20,000 abstracts** retrieved from databases (PubMed, Embase, Cochrane, CINAHL, etc.). Reviewers must manually screen each one at ~60 seconds per citation. For 10,000 abstracts with two reviewers, this alone represents **~333 person-hours** (~$25,000 at $75/hour). The "precision problem" is structural: maximizing recall (not missing relevant studies) forces overly broad searches, creating massive manual screening burdens.

**Source:** https://www.imohealth.com/resources/the-precision-problem-in-systematic-literature-review/

#### Manual Steps Across the Entire Workflow
Every SLR stage is labor-intensive:
1. **Search strategy development** — defining PICO framework (Population, Intervention, Comparator, Outcome), identifying synonyms, creating Boolean queries across 5–10 databases
2. **Abstract screening** — dual independent review of thousands of citations (60s/abstract × 10,000 = 167 hours per reviewer)
3. **Full-text review** — reading and coding included papers (15–60 min/paper × 100–500 papers)
4. **Data extraction** — extracting quantitative outcomes, baseline characteristics, safety data from tables/figures
5. **Risk of bias assessment** — applying Cochrane RoB2, GRADE, or other tools to each study
6. **Synthesis and meta-analysis** — statistical pooling, heterogeneity assessment, NMA if relevant
7. **Report writing** — PRISMA-compliant documentation, tables, forest plots
8. **Quality check and regulatory formatting** — audit trail, compliance documentation for FDA/HTA

Steps 3–7 cannot currently be automated with high confidence due to accuracy requirements in regulated contexts.

#### Regulatory-Grade Requirements Add Compliance Overhead
For FDA regulatory submissions and HTA dossiers, SLRs must meet significantly higher standards than academic reviews:
- **Dual independent review** at every stage (required for bias protection)
- **Complete audit trail** of all decisions with timestamps and reviewer identification
- **Protocol pre-registration** (PROSPERO or equivalent)
- **PRISMA reporting standards** and flow diagrams
- **Specific database coverage** requirements (no shortcuts)
- **No AI output without human verification** under current FDA guidance — AI is still in "assist" mode for regulatory use
- **Reproducibility** — every decision must be defensible during regulatory review

This compliance overhead adds 20–40% to total effort compared to an academic review.

#### Language and Terminology Variability
The same disease, drug, or outcome may be described differently across thousands of publications:
- Evolving clinical definitions (DSM-IV vs. DSM-5 for psychiatric diagnoses)
- Regional naming conventions (brand names vs. generic names)
- Abbreviations and acronyms
- Multi-lingual literature requirements (global HTA submissions require non-English sources)

Traditional keyword search strategies struggle with this. NLP/semantic search offers major improvements.

#### Update Burden (Living SLRs)
Products requiring continuous evidence monitoring (post-market surveillance, living HTA submissions, pharmacovigilance) need SLR updates every 6–24 months. Each update requires re-running the search, screening new records, extracting new data, and updating syntheses — a perpetual cost driver. A single product over its lifecycle may require 5–15 SLR updates.

#### Cost Drivers Summary
| Driver | Estimated Contribution to Total Cost |
|--------|--------------------------------------|
| Abstract screening (dual review) | 20–30% |
| Full-text review and data extraction | 30–40% |
| Risk of bias assessment | 10–15% |
| Statistical synthesis / NMA | 10–20% |
| Report writing and regulatory formatting | 10–20% |
| Project management and quality control | 5–15% |

### 5.2 FDA Regulatory Evidence Requirements

#### NDA/BLA (Drugs/Biologics)
The FDA requires, under 21 CFR 314.50 and the eCTD format:
- **Clinical Overview (Module 2.5):** ~30-page synthesis of all clinical evidence (effectively a narrative meta-analysis)
- **Clinical Summary (Module 2.7):** Detailed tabulations and analysis of all clinical trials; integrated summaries of efficacy (ISE) and safety (ISS)
- **Literature review:** "A description and analysis of any other data or information relevant to an evaluation of the safety and effectiveness of the drug product obtained or otherwise received by the applicant from any source, foreign or domestic, including information derived from clinical investigations, including controlled and uncontrolled studies" (21 CFR 314.50)

For AI tools used in submission evidence, the FDA now requires an **AI Validation Evidence Package** documenting context of use, training data, validation results, audit trails, and risk assessments — adding compliance cost when AI is used.

**Sources:**  
https://www.fda.gov/media/85056/download  
https://intuitionlabs.ai/articles/pharma-ai-validation-evidence-fda-ema

#### HTA Bodies (NICE, CADTH, ICER)
HTA submissions typically require:
- 3 separate SLRs: clinical evidence, economic evidence, health-related quality of life (HRQoL)
- Cost-effectiveness model (cost-utility analysis)
- Budget impact analysis
- Patient perspective summary

**Source:** https://intuitionlabs.ai/articles/hta-dossier-submission-guide | https://remapconsulting.com/nice/developing-a-sta-hst-submission-to-nice/

### 5.3 Where AI Has the Most Leverage

Based on the Frontiers/Pharmacology meta-review (2025, 25 studies analyzed):

| Stage | Current AI Capability | Efficiency Gain |
|-------|----------------------|-----------------|
| **Abstract screening** | High (ML + NLP well-validated) | 36%–99% time reduction; 55%–64% fewer abstracts needing human review |
| **Full-text screening** | Moderate-high | 5–6x decrease in time (replicate NMA: 2 minutes vs. hours) |
| **Data extraction (text)** | Moderate (emerging) | Significant; LLMs can extract structured data from unstructured text |
| **Data extraction (tables/figures)** | Low-moderate (active R&D) | NIH SBIR grants funding this specific capability |
| **Risk of bias assessment** | Low (high accuracy needed) | Emerging; smart critical appraisal tools in beta |
| **Statistical synthesis / NMA** | Low-moderate | Automation of standard network meta-analyses feasible for structured data |
| **Report writing** | Moderate | First drafts, PRISMA flow diagrams, structured tables |
| **Search strategy optimization** | Moderate | Semantic search > keyword search |

**Best documented labor savings (Cichewicz et al., 2023):**
- Dual-screen reviews with AI as single screener: **>75% labor reduction; 73%–91% cost savings**
- Single-screen with AI as second reviewer: **33% labor reduction; 36%–45% cost savings**

**Source:** https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/pharmacology/articles/10.3389/fphar.2025.1454245/full

**Key constraint:** The "regulatory last mile" — formal compliance, documentation, and quality control — requires humans. AI tools today are positioned as assistants that enable smaller teams to do more, not as full replacements. The regulatory-grade approval for AI-generated evidence is still evolving at FDA/EMA.

## 6. The 10x Opportunity

### Current State
- A full SLR for regulatory/HTA submission: **$150,000–$500,000**; **9–18 months**
- Primary bottleneck: manual screening and data extraction (~50–70% of total effort)
- Secondary bottleneck: regulatory compliance documentation, dual-review requirements, audit trails

### With 10x Cost Reduction + 10x Speed Improvement

| Metric | Current (Manual) | 10x Improved (AI-Augmented) | Value |
|--------|-----------------|----------------------------|-------|
| Cost per SLR | $150K–$500K | $15K–$50K | $135K–$450K saved per review |
| Time to complete | 9–18 months | 1–2 months | 7–16 months saved |
| NPV value of time saved | — | $25M–$40M per month × 7–16 months | **$175M–$640M per asset** |
| Team size required | 5–10 researchers | 1–2 researchers | 3–8 FTEs freed |

### Revenue Model Implications

**For a SaaS tool targeting pharma companies:**
- If a tool costs $50K/review (vs. $200K manual) → $150K savings per review
- A mid-size pharma company doing 20 reviews/year → $3M in annual savings
- Willingness to pay for the tool: even at 20% of savings = **$600K/year per company**
- With 100 mid-size pharma customers at $300K/year average = **$30M ARR**
- With 500 customers (academic institutions, CROs, small biotech) at $50K/year average = additional **$25M ARR**

**For a services-augmented model (like DistillerSR's managed services):**
- Human-in-the-loop AI-augmented review at $50K–$100K (vs. $200K+ manual)
- Gross margin potential: 60–70% (vs. 30–40% for pure consulting)

### The Regulatory Adoption Constraint

The principal risk to 10x speed improvement is regulatory acceptance of AI-generated evidence. Current FDA stance:
- AI tools accepted as decision-support in submissions
- AI-generated outputs require human review and documentation
- AI validation evidence packages add compliance overhead

The regulatory acceptance curve typically lags technology by 3–5 years. Companies building explainable, audit-ready, human-in-the-loop AI tools have the clearest path to regulatory adoption.

## 8. Key Takeaways

1. **The market is real and large:** $3–8B in evidence synthesis spend annually, with most of it locked in highly manual, expensive CRO and in-house workflows. Software captures only ~$154M today — a massive gap between the problem's cost and the software solutions' revenue.

2. **Cost and time estimates are well-documented:** $141K–$500K per SLR; 9–18 months. Not speculative — peer-reviewed publications quantify this.

3. **AI efficiency gains are substantial and published:** 36%–99% time reduction in abstract screening; 73%–91% cost savings in best-case dual-review automation (Cichewicz et al., 2023). This is not theoretical.

4. **The regulatory context is the critical differentiator:** Most AI tools target academics. The pharma/regulatory buyer needs audit trails, reproducibility, dual-review compliance, and FDA/HTA alignment — and pays 10–100x more per seat. DistillerSR and IQVIA understand this; most AI startups do not.

5. **Competition at the software layer is fragmented and under-funded:** The category leader (DistillerSR) has only $20M in funding. Elicit is the best-funded AI entrant at $31M/$100M valuation, but is not yet fully pharma/regulatory-grade.

6. **The strategic opportunity:** Build the regulated-industry version of Elicit — audit-ready, FDA-compliant, with AI that cuts costs by 10x and time by 5–10x, targeting the $150K–$500K per-review market, not the academic $240 per review market.

7. **CRO partnerships/competition:** IQVIA, Parexel, and Syneos are both potential customers (they need tools to augment their teams) and potential competitors (they can bundle evidence synthesis as a service). The acqui-hire of Evidinno by UBC (Jan 2026) signals CRO consolidation in this space.

8. **FDA submission timing creates massive willingness to pay:** McKinsey's estimate that each month of acceleration = $25M–$40M NPV for a late-stage asset means that 3 months faster = $75M–$120M in value. Even a $1M/year tool subscription is rounding error against this value.
